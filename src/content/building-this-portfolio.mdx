## Building This Portfolio

If you are reading this, you are looking at a website I built in about a week. Not a weekend hack. Not a template I customized. I sat down, planned it out, and built it from scratch over five to seven days.

This is my first portfolio site. For a long time, I did not have one. Most of my friends did, but I never felt like I had enough content to justify it. I had a few projects, nothing I felt was portfolio-worthy. But now I have two months of vacation, I need an internship, and I need to start marketing myself. So here we are.

## The Vibe: Terminal Meets Editorial

I originally wanted my portfolio to feel like running `ssh kiran@portfolio` and getting a terminal interface. That is not really practical on the web, so I went with a terminal aesthetic instead — monospace fonts, dark background, precise layout.

But a plain black terminal does not really work as a website. It feels like a hacker cliché. So I added an editorial layer — serif fonts for headings, generous whitespace, intentional negative space. Think of it as a technical journal that someone designed obsessively.

Minimalism is the theme, but not at the cost of personality.

## The Tech Stack

I went with **TanStack Router** for file-based routing, **Vite** for the build tool, **Tailwind CSS v4** for styling, and **Motion** for animations. Blog posts are in **MDX**. Everything runs on **Bun**.

Why TanStack Router over Next.js? Next.js has become bloated for what I needed. I wanted a static site, and TanStack Router gives me file-based routing with type safety without the overhead. After building Anokha and a few other projects with it, TanStack + Vite just became my default frontend stack. It works well with Bun, the error messages are actually helpful, and prototyping feels like a breeze.

Tailwind v4 is simpler to set up than v3 and has everything I need. I genuinely think Tailwind is one of the best things to happen to frontend development.

For animations, I used **Motion** instead of CSS or GSAP. I tried GSAP on my Anokha project and found it too verbose. CSS animations are fine, but I am not good enough to make spring-physics-level animations with pure CSS. Motion simplifies the interface — I think about _what_ I want to animate, not _how_ the CSS works.

I chose **MDX** for blog posts because building a CMS for a section I am just starting to write is overkill. Plain markdown is a pain to render. MDX lets me write in markdown with the option to drop in React components when I need them. Plus, I had never used it before and wanted to try it out.

## The Build

It took about a week, spread across multiple sessions. Planning, designing, coding, adding CI, and deploying.

The hardest part was setting up MDX rendering for the blog. I had never worked with it before, and getting the pipeline right — from file globbing to component overrides for code blocks and blockquotes — took some figuring out. It was not _hard_, but it was the most unfamiliar piece. When I finally saw my first blog post rendering properly, it was pretty satisfying.

The thing that took longer than expected was the library page. I read a decent amount of books — CS books, psychology, fiction — and I was not sure how to frame it. Should it be a bookshelf? Should it include research papers? Articles I have read? I landed on "library" because it can hold anything I have read, not just books. I have bigger plans for it — maybe an interactive bookshelf with clickable spines — but for now, it is a clean catalog with personal notes.

I am not really a frontend person. I can do frontend, but I would not call myself good at it. When I got stuck, I searched for inspiration on sites like Mobbin and looked at how other developer portfolios handled similar problems.

## Design Decisions

**Dark-only.** I am a strong believer that developers are dark mode only. I chose the dark side. There is no light theme because I never wanted one.

**The mobile warning screen.** When I was building this, I was genuinely unsure about how responsive the site would be. I built it on a PC, tested it on a PC, and I wanted to be upfront with visitors that the experience is best on a wider screen. The warning was also an experiment — my first time building something like that, and I wanted to see if I could pull it off.

**"What I Did Not Finish."** I borrowed this idea from another developer's portfolio. Most side projects in CS do not get completed. That is just the truth. But the ones that do sometimes become great open-source projects, and even the failed ones teach you something. I wanted to show that I am okay with putting my unfinished work out there — because the process matters as much as the result.

## What I Am Most Proud Of

The **Today I Learned** section. I specifically added it so I would be forced to think more deeply about what I am building. When I learn something new or solve a tricky bug, I know I have a place to put it. It makes me pay more attention, reflect more, and hopefully get better at what I do. If this section makes me think twice before moving on from a problem, it has already done its job.

## What I Would Do Differently

The hero section is too simple. I know minimalism is the theme, but this is also a frontend project, and I want to show that I can do more than a centered heading. If I build a V2, I will definitely add more to it.

## Did I Use AI?

Yes. I will not pretend I did not. AI gave me boilerplate code, handled the simple parts, and let me focus on the hard decisions. That is the only reason I finished this in a week. I am not ashamed of that — it is a tool, and I used it to move faster on the things that actually required my judgment.

## What I Learned

Not a ton of new technology — I have used most of this stack before. The two things I actually learned were **MDX** (which I had never touched) and the **command palette** search pattern. I have always wanted to build a `Cmd+K` search bar but never had the right project for it. This was the perfect excuse.

## Should You Build Your Portfolio From Scratch?

Absolutely. Building a portfolio forces you to reflect on what you have built, what you have achieved, and how you want to present yourself to the world. It is not easy. You have to think about what to showcase, what to leave out, how honest to be about your shortcomings, and where to draw the line between genuine and self-promotional.

Everyone has shortcomings. You cannot show all of them. But you also should not hide everything. A portfolio makes you negotiate that line — and that is a harder exercise than any coding challenge.
